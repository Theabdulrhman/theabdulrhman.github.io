Before this course, I genuinely thought I already had a solid grip on how the media worked. I had friends in the industry. I had even worked on a few small projects myself. A significant issue I had not realised until now is that I was only ever looking at the surface of the content I consumed. I never questioned how a shot was lit or why an advertisement was placed at a specific tram stop. Throughout this journey at Trinity College, I pushed myself to try completely new platforms and formats. This experience is relevant to me because it took me entirely out of my comfort zone. REAL LIFE  ·  FEB 2026  ·  TRINITY NEWSROOM PROJECT

Happiness
on the Menu

By Abdulrahman Alshehri, Chalalin Jirojvanichakorn & Haoming Luo

Happiness is weird. Everyone wants it and everyone posts about it, but no one explains it to us and how does it work. If you ask five random people about what happiness for them is, you most likely get five completely different answers. For some of the people it's being financially success, and for others it's being in peace. For someone else, it's canceling plans and getting more than eight hours of sleep. Happiness is something very personal which makes it both powerful and confusing.

Still, most of us grow up hearing the same thing, which is to work hard and get a good grade, build a strong career, make money and happiness will spawn in your life — it's treated like a bonus level you unlock after suffering in life. The problem is that real life does not always follow that script.

All This Success… Now Why Am I Tired?

People reach far away in their life exactly where they have dreamed of and still feel anxious, burned out, or weirdly empty. That gap shows a weird thing — if success does somehow create happiness, why are we not happy?

For a long time, happiness has been treated as something soft or secondary, almost like a side topic. Universities focus on developing skills, knowledge, and professional competence — all of them are important. But emotional wellbeing is often left behind for students to figure out on their own.

Even top Universities have begun to recognize this gap. At Yale University, psychology professor Laurie Santos created a class called The Science of Well-Being. You would expect students at one of the highest ranking universities around the world to care much more about finance, law, medicine, or politics. Instead, this course about happiness became one of the most popular classes in Yale's history. Many high achieving students chose to study wellbeing — these were not lazy students trying to avoid important subjects. They were ambitious, trying to understand how to live well.

This course later evolved into The Science of Wellbeing and reached millions of learners online. That level of interest is not random curiosity. It proves how much students need it. And it showed that academic success and emotional stability are not the same thing. Clearly people want tools not to only perform well, but to feel well.

"If one of the highest universities can make space for happiness in the classroom, others can too."

Happiness Isn't Soft — It's Strategic

Teaching happiness doesn't mean lowering standards or replacing important subjects. It means introducing research-based knowledge about how habits change mood, how social connections affect mental health, how constant comparison affects self-worth, and how purpose develops overtime rather than being magically discovered.

These are not just theoretical ideas — they are practical insights backed by psychology. Universities impose daily routines: study schedules, exams, workloads, social pressures, and competition. This can highly damage student sleep schedules, mental health, and physical health. Even students who graduate at the top of the class may still feel exhausted and anxious, feeling lost and disconnected from the outside world.

Building Lives, Not Just Careers

Education not only helps students prepare for careers — it also helps them know how to live meaningful and sustainable lives. A university degree is not just a ticket to a job; it guides students to learn, improve self-awareness, and even discover themselves. Even though grades are often used to measure success, they do not guarantee happiness.

Ambition and success are important — they give us motivation toward our life goals — but without balance and emotional strength, ambition can turn into burnout. Many students graduate exhausted and anxious, overthinking whether they measure up even with an impressive academic record. Academic knowledge is often not automatically connected with personal wellbeing.

This leads to the point that happiness should not be assumed to be a personal problem for students to figure out alone. It is essential for universities to add wellbeing as part of education. If added to the curriculum, it will help students prepare for life and support the emotional skills they need.

The Worm Turns —
Media Justification

By Abdulrahman Alshehri

Introduction

The film I chose is "The Worm Turns" (2026), a science fiction movie directed by the Russo Brothers. The movie is about space, aliens, and the journey of a scientist named Angela — rated MA15+ for violence, shocking scenes, and strong language. A short description: "A troubled scientist discovers a new alien lifeform, but the truth she uncovers threatens everything she believes in."

Target Audience

The main audience is people between 15–50 who enjoy space movies like Interstellar, The Martian, and Gravity. The film mainly targets teenagers who enjoy strong visuals and exciting stories. Angela is designed to be relatable — not a superhero, just a normal person dealing with stress and sadness. The movie's realism also attracts new-age sci-fi lovers, keeping viewers hooked through drama and danger.

Theatrical Movie Poster Design

A black background connotes space, darkness, and mystery — making other elements stand out. Angela is placed centre-poster to establish her as the main character, designed to look sad, lost, and worried, conveying that the film is about her struggles as much as the sci-fi elements. Shadows, dramatic lighting, and dark colours signal that something dangerous is coming — matching widespread sci-fi standards.

Poster Placement Strategies

Placement 01 — Tram Stops

Location: Tram stops in busy areas

Function: People wait at tram stops every day for a few minutes — giving them time to notice and read the poster. Bright lights keep it visible at night.

Intended Effect: Repeated exposure keeps the film in mind, increasing the chance people talk about it or look it up online.

Placement 02 — Movie Theatres

Location: Inside theatre hallways and near ticket counters

Function: Cinema visitors are already thinking about watching a movie — making them more likely to pay attention.

Intended Effect: Encourages future ticket purchases and helps people remember the film on their next cinema visit.

Placement 03 — Social Media

Location: Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, and Facebook

Function: The poster appears as ads, posts, or stories — spreading quickly to many people even if they are not sci-fi fans.

Intended Effect: Reaches a global audience across all age groups, creates conversation and shares, and attracts viewers who would never see a physical poster.

Conclusion

The purpose of the film marketing is to attract a large audience and create strong interest. Through a dramatic and emotional poster placed in busy public areas, cinemas, and online platforms, the marketing will reach many people quickly. When potential audiences see the poster repeatedly, they will remember the film, feel curious about Angela's story, and want to discover what happens in The Worm Turns.
